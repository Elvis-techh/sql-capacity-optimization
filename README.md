# Smart Inventory Consolidation Algorithm (Lean Six Sigma Yellow Belt)

**Role:** Data Analyst & Lean Six Sigma Lead  
**Timeline:** 2025 – 2026  
*(Note: Proprietary data and exact warehouse metrics have been sanitized for public portfolio display).*

---

## 🚨 The Problem
As the logistics facility approached peak capacity, overall storage density began to plummet due to fragmented inventory. Hundreds of partial bins were holding small quantities of identical SKUs spread across multiple aisles. 

This fragmentation created a massive opportunity cost. Valuable rack space was being artificially consumed by half-empty pallets, forcing the business into a perceived capacity shortage and increasing the labor overhead required to pick and stow items.

## 💡 The Solution (Data-Driven Consolidation)
As part of my Lean Six Sigma Yellow Belt certification, I designed a SQL-based logic engine to automatically identify and resolve storage fragmentation.

* **Targeted Algorithm:** I wrote complex SQL queries against the live enterprise database to identify underutilized storage locations and mathematically pair them for consolidation. 
* **Optimized Labor Directives:** Instead of blind audits, the algorithm generated precise source-to-destination move sheets. It calculated exactly which inventory transfers would free up the maximum amount of full pallet locations with the fewest amount of labor touches.

## 🛠 The Tech Stack
* **Languages & Tools:** SQL (Google BigQuery), Advanced Excel / Google Sheets
* **Methodologies:** Lean Six Sigma (DMAIC Framework), Process Optimization, ROI Forecasting

## 📈 The Business Impact (The ROI)
* **Massive Value Creation:** The consolidation logic was projected to free up enough physical rack space to unlock over **$400,000** in annualized storage value.
* **Incredible Cost Efficiency:** Achieved these massive capacity gains and optimized labor routing with less than **$5,000** in out-of-pocket operational costs.
* **Standardized Operations:** Proved that data-driven, proactive consolidation is significantly cheaper and more efficient than reactive warehouse expansion.
